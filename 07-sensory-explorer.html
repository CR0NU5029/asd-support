Sensory Differences in Autism

Every child on the autism spectrum experiences the world through their senses differently. Explore each sense to understand what your child might be experiencing — and what can help.

📉

Hypo-sensitive (Under)

The brain receives less sensory information than typical. The child may seek out more stimulation to feel a response — louder sounds, stronger smells, tighter hugs.

📈

Hyper-sensitive (Over)

The brain receives more sensory information than it can handle. The child may be overwhelmed by everyday sights, sounds, textures or smells that others barely notice.

A child can be hypo-sensitive in some senses and hyper-sensitive in others. This can also vary day-to-day depending on anxiety and stress levels.

👁️
Sight
How visual information is processed

👁️ What they might experience

  • Objects can appear dark and lose some of their features
  • Central vision may be blurred, but peripheral vision is sharp
  • A central object may appear magnified while surroundings blur
  • Poor depth perception — difficulties with throwing and catching

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Use visual supports — photographs, written words, real objects, coloured pictures
  • Coloured lenses can help with central vision issues
  • Practise throwing and catching as part of a daily routine to build the skill

👁️ What they might experience

  • Objects and bright lights can appear to jump or move around
  • Images can fragment or appear distorted
  • Easier to focus on small details rather than whole objects
  • Difficulty sleeping due to sensitivity to light

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Reduce bright lights in the environment where possible
  • Use sunglasses outdoors and blackout curtains for sleep
  • Use screens or partitions to block visual distractions — especially useful in classrooms
👂
Sound
How auditory information is processed

👂 What they might experience

  • May only hear sounds through one ear
  • May not acknowledge or respond to certain sounds
  • Can enjoy loud music and noisy places
  • Often talks loudly; may bang doors and objects

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Use visual supports alongside verbal communication
  • Ensure others know to communicate appropriately (face-to-face, clearly)
  • Include enjoyable, noisy activities in daily routines to meet their sensory needs

👂 What they might experience

  • Noise can be magnified — sounds become distorted or painfully loud
  • Can hear distant sounds clearly when others cannot
  • Unable to filter background noise, affecting concentration
  • Some sounds can be physically painful

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Shut doors and windows to reduce external noise where possible
  • Add carpets and soft furnishings to muffle harsh sounds in large rooms
  • Use ear plugs or music through headphones if appropriate
  • Prepare your child before entering noisy environments
👃
Smell
How olfactory information is processed

👃 What they might experience

  • Little or no sense of smell
  • Failure to recognise strong odours, including their own body odour
  • May actively seek out strong, unusual smells

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Create a fun routine that includes attention to personal hygiene
  • Use strong-smelling products to distract from inappropriate smell-seeking
  • Use favourite strong smells as rewards for positive behaviour

👃 What they might experience

  • Smells can feel intense and overwhelming
  • May dislike people wearing perfume, strong shampoos or deodorant
  • May avoid places like public toilets, canteens or restaurants

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Switch to unscented cleaning products, detergents and toiletries
  • Avoid wearing perfume around your child
  • Keep the home as fragrance-free as possible
  • Use a favourite subtle smell (on a tissue) to distract from strong scents and build tolerance gradually
👅
Taste
How gustatory information is processed

👅 What they might experience

  • Seeks out very spicy or intensely flavoured foods
  • May eat non-edible items such as stones, grass, clay or metal (known as pica)
  • May need very strong flavours to register taste at all

✅ Strategies that can help

  • If diet has some variety, it may not be a clinical concern — speak to a professional if worried
  • Introduce new foods very gradually, in tiny steps
  • Involve your child in food preparation to increase familiarity
  • Use reward charts for trying new foods

👅 What they might experience

  • Finds many flavours too strong and overwhelming
  • Very sensitive taste buds — foods that seem mild to others can feel intense
  • Often has a restricted diet, preferring bland or familiar foods

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Take small steps — introduce new foods alongside familiar safe foods
  • Use visual supports (pictures of food) to provide information and reduce anxiety about meals
  • Set mealtimes and menu planning give comforting structure and routine
  • Change food textures if needed (e.g. pureeing or blending) and introduce variety slowly
🤚
Touch
How tactile information is processed — including heat, cold and pain

🤚 What they might experience

  • May hold others very tightly — seeking deep pressure
  • High pain threshold — may not react to injuries that would hurt others
  • May not feel food properly in the mouth
  • May enjoy heavy objects, weighted items, or tight clothing
  • May chew clothing, hair or inedible objects for sensory input
  • May enjoy textures others find unpleasant (smearing, touching different surfaces)

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Use weighted blankets or sleeping bags to provide calming deep pressure
  • Provide appropriate objects to satisfy tactile needs (jelly, water play, textured toys)
  • Offer appropriate chew toys (available from specialist suppliers) as a safe alternative

🤚 What they might experience

  • Touch can be painful or deeply uncomfortable
  • May dislike objects on hands or feet (socks, shoes, tags)
  • Difficulties with hair brushing, washing or nail cutting
  • Food textures may feel unbearable
  • Can only tolerate specific types of clothing (seams, labels, fabric type)

✅ Strategies that can help

  • Always warn your child before touching them — approach from the front
  • Remember: a hug may feel painful rather than comforting — follow their lead
  • Remove all tags and labels from clothing
  • Allow them to wear clothes they feel comfortable in where possible
  • Gradually introduce different textures with a box of materials — reward new experiences
  • Change food textures (e.g. puree) rather than forcing the original texture

💡 Good to Know

🔄

It can change daily. Sensory sensitivities can vary depending on your child's anxiety levels, tiredness and stress on any given day.

🧩

Every child is different. It's impossible to predict how sensory issues will impact each individual — observe and learn your child's unique profile.

🔎

Behaviour is communication. Many "difficult" behaviours are actually a response to sensory overwhelm. Understanding the source helps you respond better.

📋

Sensory profiling questionnaires can help identify your child's specific sensory issues. Find them at autismeducationtrust.org.uk
