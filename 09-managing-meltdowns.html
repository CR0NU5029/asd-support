Managing Meltdowns

A calm, practical guide for parents and carers — before, during and after

💛 First Things to Remember

🚨

A meltdown is best understood as a panic attack — not a choice, not a battle to win.

🧠

During a meltdown, the child cannot learn. Traditional discipline won't work here.

❤️

You are your child's emotional brakes. How you respond makes a real difference.

🔁

Separate your relationship with your child from the behaviour you're seeing.

🛡️

Emergency rule: Don't hurt yourself, others, or damage property. Safety first, always.

💬

It's "can't" not "won't" — they are genuinely unable to control this in the moment.

⚠️ Prevention — Spotting the Signs Early

Prevention is far better than cure. Watch for rising anxiety before things escalate.

👀
Watch for triggers

Be aware of the situations, places, sounds or events that have caused meltdowns before.

📈
Spot rising anxiety

Look for changes in body language, increased stimming, quietness, or repetitive questions — these can be early signals.

📅
Plan for tricky situations

If you know a noisy event or change of routine is coming, prepare your child in advance with clear, gentle information.

🌡️
Track good days and hard days

Anxiety levels vary daily. On harder days, reduce demands and expectations to lower the pressure.

🏠
Have a safe place ready

Know in advance where your child can go to decompress — a bedroom, a quiet corner, a den — before things escalate.

🧩
Build a toolkit

Know what helps your child calm — headphones, weighted blankets, a favourite item. Have these accessible.

🔥 During a Meltdown — In the Moment

Your calmness is the most powerful tool you have right now.

😌
Stay calm and neutral

Keep your voice level, face neutral and body language open. Shouting or showing frustration will escalate things.

📉
Reduce stimulation

Turn down the TV, ask others to be quiet, reduce noise and visual busyness where possible.

🤫
Keep communication minimal

Short, simple instructions only. Now is not the time for explanation, reasoning or negotiation.

↔️
Give space

Step back rather than forward. Give physical and emotional space — don't crowd or corner them.

🚪
Encourage exit to safety

Guide your child to their safe space — bedroom, garden, quiet corner. Guide rather than force if possible.

✅
Steer positively

Rather than "stop running around", try "you might feel calmer if you stand still" — suggest, don't command.

🏃
If they run

If safe to do so, following at a distance is better than chasing — chasing can increase panic.

🤗
When calming begins

Once the peak passes, offer verbal reassurance. Younger children may welcome "would you like a hug until you feel better?"

🚫

Things to avoid during a meltdown

  • Don't threaten punishments — the child cannot respond to these right now
  • Don't offer rewards — same reason, they're beyond reasoning in this state
  • Don't raise your voice — it adds to the sensory overwhelm
  • Don't force physical contact — touch can feel painful when overwhelmed
  • Don't involve other people unnecessarily — an audience makes things worse

🌱 After a Meltdown — Recovery

Recovery time is real. Don't rush past this phase.

⏳
Allow recovery time

Children need time to recompose after a meltdown. Their safe place is a good spot for this.

🍎
Offer a snack or drink

A small snack or drink can genuinely help a child calm down and recover physically.

💛
Reassure them

They may need to hear that it's over, and that you still love and like them. "I know you couldn't help that" goes a long way.

🔄
Fresh start

Try to move forward without dwelling on the incident. Starting fresh helps both of you.

💬
Consider a debrief — carefully

Some children find it helpful to talk about what happened later. But be cautious — for many, this can trigger another meltdown. Know your child.

📓
Note any patterns

Reflecting on what triggered this — quietly, for yourself — can help you prevent or manage the next one better.

🏠 Home Strategies

⚖️

Choose Your Battles

Ask: is this worth a massive meltdown? Flexibility at home matters.

Save firm boundaries for what genuinely matters — safety, harm. Let smaller things go when anxiety is high.

Balance Tolerance & Demands

Match expectations to the day — not every day is the same.

On hard days, reduce demands. On easier days, gently increase them. Go with the flow of their anxiety levels.

🎯

Indirect Praise

Direct praise can feel overwhelming — try a gentler approach.

"I was just telling Grandma how brilliantly you helped me earlier" (said while they're in earshot) — much easier to accept.

🎮

Indirect Commands

Turn demands into challenges or invitations.

"Bet I can get my coat on before you!" or "Can you show me how you do that?" works far better than "Put your coat on."

🤷

Pretend You Don't Know

Getting it wrong on purpose gives them control.

Misread a word in a book, or ask "Could you show me how you do this?" for something you want them to do independently.

🔀

Offer Limited Choices

Give some control without losing the outcome you need.

"Do you want to start your homework at 3pm or 4pm?" — either way, it gets done; they feel in control.

When… Then

A simple, positive structure for expectations.

"When you've done your homework, then you can play Xbox." Keep it calm and consistent — not a threat, just information.

Fresh Start Every Day

Don't let yesterday follow you into today.

Whatever happened yesterday, start fresh. Carrying it over adds to anxiety levels and makes a meltdown more likely today.
