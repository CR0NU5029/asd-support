Understanding & Managing Behaviour

Why do children with ASD display certain behaviours — and what can you do? Explore the roots of behaviour and discover practical strategies to help.

🌱 The Roots of Behaviour

Every behaviour has a reason. For children with ASD, difficult behaviour usually traces back to one or more of these four areas. Tap each one to learn more.

💬

Communication

Difficulty expressing needs or understanding others

  • May struggle to use language to communicate needs
  • Can speak very loudly or find non-verbal cues hard
  • May understand things very literally
  • Difficult behaviour is often the only way they can get a need met when words fail
Tap to expand
👥

Social Interaction

Challenges with two-way social engagement

  • May avoid social contact or be unaware of social rules
  • Can become anxious and distressed in social situations
  • May not understand personal space or turn-taking
  • Anxiety from social demands can trigger difficult behaviours
Tap to expand
🔁

Routine & Repetition

Need for predictability and sameness

  • Often prefers routine and struggles with unexpected changes
  • May focus intensely on small details rather than the bigger picture
  • May struggle to see things from another's perspective
  • A change to the usual routine is a very common trigger for difficult behaviour
Tap to expand
🌡️

Sensory Processing

Over or under sensitivity to sensory information

  • Related to vision, hearing, smell, touch and taste
  • Sensory overload can look like "naughtiness" when it's actually overwhelm
  • Environment plays a big role — light, noise, temperature all matter
  • Reducing sensory triggers can dramatically reduce difficult behaviour

⭐ The STAR Analysis Tool

When a behaviour keeps happening, use the STAR framework to investigate. It assumes every behaviour serves a purpose — your job is to figure out what that purpose is. Tap each area to explore.

S

Settings

Where and when does the behaviour happen?

Consider the environment carefully. What is the light like? Is it noisy? Is it crowded? Is the child unwell or tired? Physical and environmental factors set the scene for what follows.
Tap to see detail ▾
T

Triggers

What sets the behaviour off?

Look for what happened immediately before the behaviour. Triggers can build up throughout the day — what seems small might be "the last straw." Keep a diary to spot patterns over time.
Tap to see detail ▾
A

Actions

Exactly what does the behaviour look like?

Describe the behaviour precisely — not "they were naughty" but "they threw the cup, screamed for 3 minutes, then lay on the floor." Precision helps you spot what's really happening.
Tap to see detail ▾
R

Results

What happens afterwards?

What follows the behaviour determines whether it's likely to happen again. If the behaviour "works" (they get what they want, escape something they don't like, or get attention), it will be repeated. Changing the result can change the behaviour.

🛠️ Your Strategy Toolkit

There's no single answer — different strategies work at different times. Explore the categories below to find what might help your child.

🔕
Reduce stimulation

Keep your child's environment calm with low levels of noise, bright lights and busyness where possible.

📅
Visual timetables

A visual timetable helps your child understand what's coming next, reducing anxiety about transitions and change. Free templates at do2learn.co.uk

🎯
Predictability is calming

Routine reduces the anxiety that underlies much difficult behaviour. When changes must happen, give as much advance notice as possible.

🗣️
Use simple language

Use words your child will understand. Avoid sarcasm and idioms — they may be taken literally.

➡️
Say things in order

"Put your shoes on, then we'll go to the park" — say instructions in the sequence they'll happen.

🔁
Use repetition

Repetition helps with memory and processing. Don't expect something to "stick" the first time it's said.

📖
Social Stories

Short descriptions of situations your child finds difficult, explaining what to expect and why. Visit letkidsdream.webs.com for examples.

👋
Teach social skills directly

Don't assume — teach things like personal space, appropriate topics and non-verbal cues explicitly. Contact CHAPS (Cheshire Autism Practical Support) for further help.

📊
Emotion scales

Visual anger scales, arousal charts and facial expression charts help children identify and name their feelings. Find resources at do2learn.co.uk

🎵
Anger management techniques

Counting exercises, listening to music, and learning to walk away are all valuable skills to teach and practise.

🧘
Relaxation strategies

Deep breathing, massage, calming activities and positive thinking can all help your child manage anger and anxiety.

🏃
Physical activity

Exercise is a powerful regulator of anxiety and mood. Building regular physical activity into the day can make a significant difference.

🌟
Positive Reinforcement

Reward the behaviour you want to see. Keep rewards small, achievable and varied. Verbal praise that describes the specific behaviour works well too.

📋
Reward charts

Visual charts can be very motivating. Targets should be realistic and achievable — early success builds momentum. Free charts at freeprintablebehaviourcharts.com

🆕
Focus on new skills

The goal isn't just to stop a behaviour — it's to give your child other ways to get what they need. Always think: "what skill can I replace this with?"

🎯
One or two at a time

When making changes, focus on one or two behaviours only — the ones most affecting daily life. Trying to tackle everything at once is overwhelming for everyone.

🙈
Planned Ignoring

Ignore the behaviour, not the child. Useful for attention-seeking behaviours — but always pair with positive reinforcement so the child learns a better way to get attention.

↩️
Redirection

Redirect your child's attention to defuse or prevent escalation. Move them physically, or shift their focus to something calming or engaging.

⚠️
Know your limits

Stop using planned ignoring immediately if the child or anyone around them is at risk of being hurt. Safety always comes first.

🔄
Be consistent

Inconsistency is one of the biggest barriers to changing behaviour. Everyone around the child needs to respond in the same way.
