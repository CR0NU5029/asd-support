Home Strategies — Deep Dive

Each of the 8 home strategies explored in depth: the why behind them, exactly how to use them, and what to watch out for. Written for real life with real children.

💛 Especially helpful for children with PDA (Pathological Demand Avoidance)

Choose Your Battles

Not every hill is worth dying on — and choosing wisely is a skill, not a surrender

💡 Why This Works

Every demand you place on an autistic child adds to their "anxiety bucket." For children with PDA (Pathological Demand Avoidance), demands are processed by the nervous system as genuine threats — triggering the same fight-or-flight response you'd have if someone pointed a gun at you. This is not defiance; it is neurology.

When the bucket overflows — and it will, because school, sensory input, and social navigation fill it up long before they get home — a meltdown happens. By reducing unnecessary demands, you keep the bucket from reaching that tipping point.

The goal isn't to have no expectations. It's to be strategic about which ones you spend your limited daily goodwill on. Battles over safety and genuine wellbeing are always worth it. Battles over whether they eat their peas at 6pm on a Tuesday usually aren't.

The autonomy-threat model (Liz O'Nions & colleagues, 2023) explains PDA as a nervous system that detects any demand as a potential loss of control — triggering anxiety before any reasoning can happen.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1Before insisting, pause and ask yourself: "What is the actual consequence if I let this go right now?"
  2. 2Sort demands into three groups: Non-negotiable (safety/harm), Important but can wait, and Honestly doesn't matter.
  3. 3On high-anxiety days, drop the "important but can wait" list entirely. Non-negotiables only.
  4. 4Communicate your system to all adults in the household — a united front prevents confusion and manipulation.
  5. 5Revisit your list every few weeks. Priorities shift as your child grows and circumstances change.
📖 Real-Life Example
👧 Ellie, age 9

Ellie arrives home from school visibly depleted — she's been "holding it together" all day. You're cooking dinner and ask her to wash her hands. She refuses flatly. The old approach: insist, escalate, meltdown, nobody eats in peace.

The battle-choosing approach: pause. Is unwashed hands before dinner a safety issue? Not really. You offer a wet wipe on the counter and say "whenever you're ready." Dinner happens without drama. Hand-washing is a conversation for a calmer evening — or not at all today. The relationship is intact. You chose the battle that mattered: dinner together, not hygiene in a crisis moment.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Don't interpret this as having no expectations — it's strategic, not a free-for-all. Children still need clear non-negotiables.
  • Avoid being inconsistent across the household. If one parent always lets things go and the other doesn't, your child will be confused and more anxious.
  • This is hard when you're exhausted too. Give yourself permission to be imperfect on this one.

Balance Tolerance & Demands

Match your expectations to the day — not every day is the same nervous system

💡 Why This Works

Dr. Daniel Siegel's "Window of Tolerance" describes the zone of emotional regulation where a person can think, feel, and respond appropriately. Autistic children typically have a narrower window than their neurotypical peers — and that window shifts significantly day to day.

Sleep quality, sensory load from school, social stress, illness, hunger, and hormones all affect how much a child can handle on any given day. Pushing full expectations onto a child whose nervous system is already near its limit doesn't build resilience — it overflows the bucket and produces the very meltdowns you're trying to prevent.

Reading the daily state of your child's window and calibrating demands accordingly isn't "giving in" — it's intelligent, informed parenting based on how their nervous system actually works.

Research by Autismunderstood.co.uk and Neurodivergent Insights confirms that autistic individuals often have a significantly narrower window of tolerance, and that co-regulation by a calm adult is the most effective way to expand it over time.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1Keep a simple daily log: Morning (green/amber/red), After school (green/amber/red), Evening. After a few weeks, patterns emerge.
  2. 2Learn your child's green signals (flexible, chatty, can-do energy) vs red signals (stimming more, quieter, easily startled, snapping).
  3. 3On red days: minimal demands. Snack, quiet time, no homework battles, basic hygiene only. Let the nervous system recover first.
  4. 4On green days: introduce harder things. New routines, tricky conversations, skill practice.
  5. 5Share the system with school — ask them to flag hard days so you're forewarned before pickup.
📖 Real-Life Example
👦 Theo, age 11

Theo comes home on a Monday afternoon — headphones in, not making eye contact, goes straight to his room. His parent has learned this is a red-level day. No demands are made for the first hour. Snack left outside his room. TV on in the background.

By 6pm, Theo appears and is chatty. Parent says: "I've been thinking — when you're feeling up to it, maybe 20 minutes of homework?" Theo says "yeah okay." It gets done. Reading the day and responding to it — rather than the clock — prevented a battle that would have taken all evening.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • This is not rewarding bad behaviour — you're responding to nervous system state, not to a choice your child is making.
  • Don't confuse this with having no routine. Predictable structure is still important — it's the demands within the structure that flex.
  • It can be hard to balance this with siblings who have different needs. You don't need to apply the same rule to everyone — different children, different needs.

Indirect Praise

Letting them overhear something wonderful — so it lands without pressure

💡 Why This Works

Direct praise — "You were AMAZING at that!" — can feel threatening to an autistic or PDA child for several reasons. It implies the adult was watching and judging. It raises the stakes ("can I do that again next time?"). It may feel disproportionate or confusing if the child has alexithymia (difficulty identifying their own emotions).

Indirect praise sidesteps all of this. "Accidentally" overhearing something good about yourself carries none of those complications. There's no demand to respond, no eye contact, no assessment. The child absorbs the information privately and emotionally — and it still counts.

Phil Christie and Elizabeth Newson, pioneers of PDA understanding, specifically identify indirect praise as more effective than direct praise for demand-avoidant children. The PDA Society's guidance echoes this strongly.

PDA Society research and clinical guidance consistently recommends indirect communication of praise to reduce the demand aspect of being assessed — even a kind assessment can feel threatening.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1Say something positive to another adult (or into a phone call, or to a pet) while your child is within earshot: "I was just saying to Dad how quickly you figured that out earlier."
  2. 2Frame it as reporting fact, not evaluation: "I noticed Kai sorted those cards in exactly the right order" — not "Kai is so clever!"
  3. 3Write praise in a note and leave it somewhere they'll find it — a lunchbox, pillow, or phone message. Written praise removes the confrontation of a face-to-face moment.
  4. 4Tell a favourite toy or character: "Tell Bear about what you did today — I thought it was brilliant." This creates gentle distance.
  5. 5Avoid superlatives and pressure words: "brilliant", "incredible", "perfect". Instead: "I noticed", "I liked that", "that worked really well".
📖 Real-Life Example
👧 Mia, age 7

Mia draws a picture with real detail and care. Direct approach: "Oh my goodness that is AMAZING, you are so talented!" — Mia immediately looks anxious, crumples it up, says "it's rubbish."

Indirect approach: Mia is in the kitchen while her parent is on the phone to Grandma. Parent says casually: "Oh Mia did this drawing earlier — there's all this tiny detail in the background, I keep looking at it." Mia stands a little taller. She didn't have to respond, didn't have to perform, didn't have to face a direct assessment. The praise landed — without triggering the anxiety that direct praise would have brought.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Don't make it too theatrical or obviously staged — if it feels scripted, the child will see through it and it can backfire.
  • Not every child needs indirect praise. For some autistic children, direct warm praise is fine — this technique is specifically useful where direct praise causes withdrawal or anxiety.
  • Don't switch entirely to indirect and remove all warmth from your direct interactions. Tone still matters.

Indirect Commands

Disguise the demand so the nervous system doesn't register it as a threat

💡 Why This Works

A direct command — "Put your shoes on" — is processed by a demand-avoidant child's nervous system as an immediate threat to their autonomy. Before reasoning even begins, the fight-or-flight response activates. Refusal isn't a choice at that point; it's a neurological response.

An indirect command — "Bet I can get my coat on before you!" — never triggers that system, because it arrives as a challenge, a game, a piece of curiosity. The demand is still present, but it's wearing a disguise. The child's nervous system hears "fun challenge" rather than "threat to autonomy."

This is the "Disguise demands" component of the PANDA framework (Pick battles, Anxiety management, Negotiate, Disguise demands, Adapt), developed to support children with demand avoidance. Research from Elizabeth Newson's original PDA work shows children "responded best to novelty, humour and flexibility."

The PANDA approach (Manhattan Psychology Group, PDA Society) specifically identifies demand disguising through novelty, humour, and games as a core strategy — not manipulation, but nervous system-informed communication.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1Competition: "Bet I can get my coat on before you!" — race energy replaces demand energy.
  2. 2Wondering: "I wonder how quickly you could do that..." — curiosity, not instruction.
  3. 3Expertise: "Can you show me how? You're way better at this than me." — they're the teacher, not the one being directed.
  4. 4Narrative/mission: "The mission is: pyjamas on in under 3 minutes. I'll time you."
  5. 5Humour: Do the task wrong yourself and wait for the correction. They cannot resist fixing it.
📖 Real-Life Example
👦 Jake, age 10

Jake's room is a disaster zone and it needs sorting. Direct: "Tidy your room, please." Jake: "No. Later." Twenty minutes later: argument. Another twenty: meltdown. Nobody wins.

Indirect: Parent wanders in, starts picking up a pile, says: "I give up, I can't sort this... I reckon this pile alone would take me at least 5 minutes." Jake: "It would not, watch." He sorts it in two minutes. Parent: "I cannot believe how quickly you did that." Room improves significantly. The demand happened — but it never once felt like one.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Rotate your techniques — if you use the same approach every time, novelty wears off and it stops working.
  • If your child explicitly calls it out ("Are you trying to trick me?") — agree cheerfully. "Maybe! Did it work?" Honesty doesn't break the approach.
  • Don't use this when the child is already in a meltdown — it works on neutral ground, not crisis ground.

Pretend You Don't Know

When they're the expert, the power dynamic shifts — and so does the resistance

💡 Why This Works

Role reversal is one of the most quietly powerful tools in the demand-avoidance toolkit. When you pretend not to know something your child knows well, you hand control entirely to them. They become the teacher, the helper, the person with the answers. The demand to comply disappears — replaced with an invitation to lead.

This aligns directly with the Collaborative and Proactive Solutions (CPS) model developed by Dr. Ross Greene — the idea that involving the child in the solution gives them ownership, and ownership reduces resistance. It also creates intrinsic motivation: I want to help, rather than I have no choice but to obey.

Sabotage (getting something deliberately wrong) taps into a child's natural instinct to correct errors — especially for perfectionistic or detail-focused autistic children, who often find incorrect information genuinely uncomfortable to leave alone.

Dr. Ross Greene's CPS model (Collaborative and Proactive Solutions) is evidence-backed for reducing explosive behaviour through collaborative problem-solving and shared ownership — removing the authority-submission dynamic that triggers demand avoidance.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1Ask for genuine help with something they can do independently: "How do I log into this? I always forget."
  2. 2Make intentional mistakes and wait for the correction: misread a word in a book, put shoes on wrong feet, start a game wrong.
  3. 3Let them plan the solution: "I've no idea how to sort this — you know more about it than me. What should we do first?"
  4. 4Use this in routines: "I can never remember — is it teeth first or face wash first?"
  5. 5Sell the confusion convincingly — half-hearted pretending won't land. Commit to it.
📖 Real-Life Example
👦 Sam, age 8

Every night, teeth brushing is a battle. Direct requests result in flat refusal. One evening, Sam's parent picks up their own toothbrush, looks confused, and begins attempting to "brush" their hair with it. Then a finger. Then an elbow.

Sam watches for about five seconds before he cannot stand it: "That's not how you do it! You do it like THIS." He demonstrates. Parent continues getting it slightly wrong. Sam takes over completely and they both end up brushing teeth. The task happened because Sam was in charge of it — not because he was told to do it.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Some children find this patronising if it's obvious — especially older or more perceptive children. Adjust your approach accordingly.
  • Works best on neutral or green days. If your child is already anxious or dysregulated, the playfulness may not land.
  • Don't use this for everything — it loses magic if it's the only tool in your kit.

Offer Limited Choices

Give genuine control within safe limits — autonomy without chaos

💡 Why This Works

The core need driving demand avoidance is autonomy — not getting their own way, but feeling they have a genuine say in what happens to them. Two real choices satisfies that need without surrendering the outcome you actually need.

A 2024 study by Ryan and colleagues on autistic adults with intellectual disabilities confirmed that perceived choice significantly reduces anxiety responses. The key word is perceived — the feeling of control is often more important than actual control.

Critically, offering too many choices has the opposite effect — it creates decision fatigue and its own anxiety. Two options is the sweet spot: enough for agency, not so many as to overwhelm. For younger children or on high-anxiety days, visual choice cards (pictures rather than words) reduce the cognitive demand even further.

Ryan et al. (2024) in the journal Autism found that autonomy support — including offering meaningful choices — significantly improved wellbeing and reduced challenging behaviour in autistic individuals. (journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/13623613241254432)
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1Always offer exactly 2 real options — both genuinely acceptable to you: "Pasta or rice tonight?"
  2. 2Use choices to create pace control: "Do you want to start now or in 5 minutes?" — either answer gets the task done.
  3. 3Use choices for routine: "Shower before dinner or after?" "Shoes on first or bag packed first?"
  4. 4If they can't choose and freeze, offer a "third option" escape: "Or is there something else that would work better for you?"
  5. 5For younger children: use visual choice boards — laminated pictures of two options they can physically point to or pick up.
📖 Real-Life Example
👧 Freya, age 12

Homework is a nightly battle. Open-ended: "When are you going to do your homework?" — freezes, avoids, eventually refuses. Direct: "Do your homework now." — immediate resistance.

Limited choice: "Do you want to do maths or English first?" Freya thinks for a moment. "English." She opens her book. The choice gave her enough perceived control to begin — the homework still happens, but she decided how. Agency in the approach removed the threat of the demand itself.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Both choices must be genuine — if they choose the "wrong" one and you override it, you break trust and make future choices meaningless.
  • Don't offer choices when you're in a hurry and can only accept one outcome. If you can't honour both options, don't ask.
  • Avoid this when the child is mid-meltdown — decision-making is impossible when emotionally dysregulated. Choices work at calm baseline.

When… Then

It's not a threat — it's a map. And autistic brains love a map.

💡 Why This Works

There's an important difference between "If… then" (which sounds conditional and threatening: "If you don't tidy up, then no screen time") and "When… then" (which is sequential and predictable: "When we've tidied up, then it's screen time"). Same outcome, completely different nervous system response.

"When" implies certainty — the first thing will happen, and the good thing will follow. There's no threat, no punishment framing, no negotiation. It's simply information about what comes next. Autistic children typically rely heavily on predictability to feel safe — knowing the sequence ahead of time removes uncertainty, which is a major source of anxiety.

For younger children, visual First-Then boards (a picture of the task, then a picture of the reward) make the sequence tangible and remove the need to process verbal language under stress. These are widely used in early autism support for good reason.

Predictability and reduced uncertainty are robustly linked to reduced anxiety in autism across multiple research studies. The National Autistic Society's guidance on demand avoidance specifically recommends "when…then" framing over conditional or threatening language.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1State it once, calmly, as fact: "When you've had tea, then it's free time." Don't repeat it — repetition signals anxiety and creates pressure.
  2. 2Keep the chain short: one When, one Then. Adding more ("And then after that, you'll also need to…") overwhelms.
  3. 3Make the "then" something they genuinely want — if the reward is vague or unappealing, the structure loses its pull.
  4. 4For younger children: use a visual First-Then board — pictures, not just words. This removes language processing demands.
  5. 5Follow through every single time. The power of When-Then is in its reliability. Break it once and trust erodes fast.
📖 Real-Life Example
👦 Leo, age 9

Leo wants his iPad the moment he gets home from school. A battle used to erupt every afternoon. Now his parent meets him at the door with: "When you've had a snack and shown me your reading book, then iPad." Said once. Calmly. Not repeated.

Leo knows the map. He eats his snack, brings his reading book, and picks up his iPad within 15 minutes. No battle. No drama. The predictable structure gave his brain exactly what it needed to cooperate — a clear, reliable path to the thing he wanted.

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Never extend the chain mid-session: "...but also before that, you need to…" This breaks trust and the whole structure collapses.
  • If the "then" doesn't happen reliably (you forget, something comes up), the system stops working. Consistency is non-negotiable here.
  • Watch your wording: "If you don't do X, then Y won't happen" is a threat, not a When-Then. Threats trigger avoidance. Keep it positive and sequential.

Fresh Start Every Day

Yesterday's storm doesn't have to be today's forecast — for either of you

💡 Why This Works

Emotional memory in autistic individuals is often intense and long-lasting. The nervous system does not easily "reset" after a significant meltdown — both child and parent carry the residue. When the next morning begins with unresolved tension, the anxiety baseline is already elevated before the day has even started.

Carrying yesterday's events into today is experienced by demand-avoidant children as another layer of demand — "now I also have to deal with the emotional weight of yesterday." It adds directly to the anxiety bucket from the moment they wake up.

The Low Arousal Approach (developed by Andy McDonnell at Studio III) specifically identifies "not using past behaviour in current management" as a key principle. The research basis is co-regulation: a calm, warm, fresh-start adult helps reset the child's nervous system. A tense, watchful, still-cross adult prolongs dysregulation.

A fresh start is also a powerful model of something your child needs to be able to do themselves — let things go, move forward, not be defined by their worst moments.

The Low Arousal Approach (Studio III / Andy McDonnell) has an established evidence base in reducing restrictive practice and supporting emotional dysregulation in autism and learning disabilities. Central to it is the principle that caregiver arousal directly affects the regulated state of the person they support.
🛠️ How to Do It
  1. 1A simple morning ritual: warm greeting, favourite breakfast, ordinary conversation — as if yesterday didn't happen.
  2. 2Don't reference the meltdown at morning — let it go until both of you are calm, regulated, and genuinely ready (often hours or days later).
  3. 3If something does need addressing for safety reasons, choose a specific calm time — not first thing, not near bedtime.
  4. 4Model it out loud if it helps: "New day, fresh start. Whatever happened is done."
  5. 5Make it mutual — your child also gets to let things go about you. It's a two-way reset, not just compliance.
📖 Real-Life Example
Parent & Child, Monday/Tuesday

Monday was really hard. Things were said. A meltdown happened at dinner and went on for an hour. Everyone went to bed stressed and exhausted.

Tuesday morning: parent makes their child's favourite cereal without being asked. Puts it on the table. Says "Morning" like it's any other day. No reference to Monday. No loaded looks. No "I hope today is better" (which implies yesterday was bad — they already know). Child sits down. Eats breakfast. Shoulders drop. The fresh start wasn't permission — it was a gift. It said: "I love you more than I'm angry at what happened."

⚠️ Watch Out For
  • Fresh start doesn't mean avoiding every necessary conversation — it means timing them correctly. Safety issues still need addressing, just not first thing.
  • This is genuinely hard when you're exhausted, hurt, or still processing what happened. Self-compassion applies here. You're allowed to need a fresh start too.
  • Avoid "I hope today is better" — it carries the weight of yesterday into today implicitly. Try neutral warmth instead.
