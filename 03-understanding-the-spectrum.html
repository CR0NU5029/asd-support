Understanding the Autism Spectrum

The Spikey Profile — a much better way to understand how autism actually works

🔄 A Better Way to Think About Autism

For years, people imagined autism as a simple scale from "mild" to "severe". This picture is misleading — and it's time to replace it.

❌ Old thinking — the linear scale

MildModerateSevere

This suggests everyone fits neatly on a single line. It doesn't explain why the same person can be brilliant at one thing and struggle enormously with another.

⚠️ Problem: "They seem fine, they can't be that autistic" — this thinking causes real harm when support needs are dismissed.

✅ New thinking — the spikey profile

Each person has their own unique shape — high in some areas, lower in others. The same person can be exceptionally strong in one dimension and need significant support in another.

✅ This explains why autistic people are so different from each other — no two profiles are the same!

💡 Key Insights for Parents

🎭

Masking can hide the profile

Some children work very hard to appear "normal" at school, hiding their struggles. They may seem fine to teachers but be exhausted at home. The real profile might be harder than it looks.

📅

Profiles change over time

The same child's profile can look different as they grow, receive support, and develop. Strengths can increase; areas that were challenging may become easier — and vice versa.

🌡️

Anxiety changes everything

On high-anxiety days, the profile can look much more "spikey". A child who usually copes may suddenly find everything overwhelming when anxious or tired.

⭐

High spikes are strengths too

Areas where your child is exceptionally strong — a deep special interest, remarkable memory, or intense focus — are just as much part of their profile as their challenges. These are real gifts.

🤝

Share the profile with school

Helping teachers understand your child's specific profile — rather than just saying "autistic" — can dramatically improve the support they receive. A Patient Passport can help with this.

❤️

Autism doesn't define them

The profile sits inside your child's unique personality. An outgoing child with autism will still want connection — they'll just need more support navigating it. The profile shapes how, not who.
